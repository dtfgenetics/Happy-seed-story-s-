# Page 27 - What We Learned

Benny’s story shows germination.

Germination is when a seed begins to grow. Many seeds begin germination when they have moisture, oxygen, a suitable temperature, and time.

Water can help a seed swell and begin changing. The seed coat may soften or crack. In many common seeds, the first tiny root, called the radicle, appears before the shoot.

The root grows downward. The shoot grows upward. After leaves emerge, light becomes important for the young plant.

Not all seeds need the exact same conditions. Some seeds need special conditions before they begin, so scientists observe carefully.

# Page 28 - Words That Grew

Seed: a tiny plant beginning that can grow when conditions are right.

Germination: when a seed begins to grow.

Seed coat: the outer covering that helps protect a seed.

Moisture: a small amount of water.

Oxygen: a gas in air that living things need.

Suitable temperature: a temperature that is right for a seed to begin growing.

Radicle: the first tiny root that often grows from a seed.

Root: the plant part that usually grows downward and helps hold the plant.

Shoot: the young plant part that grows upward.

Sprout: a young plant that has just begun to grow.

Observe: to look carefully and notice changes.

# Page 29 - Try It Yourself: Watch a Bean Wake Up

You will need:

- a dry bean seed
- a clear cup or clear bag
- a paper towel
- water
- crayons or pencil

Steps:

1. Dampen the paper towel. It should be damp, not dripping.
2. Place the bean seed against the towel.
3. Put the towel and seed in a clear cup or bag.
4. Check it once a day.
5. Draw what you see.

Look for swelling, cracking, a first root, a shoot, and changes over time.

What may happen:

Some seeds swell quickly. Some take longer. Some may not sprout if the conditions are not right. That is still something to observe.

Safety: Do not eat experiment seeds. Wash hands after handling seeds or plant materials. Throw away anything that becomes moldy.

# Page 31 - Parent / Teacher Guide

Read-aloud focus:

Benny feels impatient because he cannot see himself growing yet. Invite children to notice how his feelings change as he discovers hidden progress.

Discussion questions:

1. Why did Benny think nothing was happening?
2. What helped Benny begin changing?
3. What appeared first, the root or the shoot?
4. Why did Benny still need patience?
5. When have you had to wait for something important?

Science note:

Many seeds begin germination with moisture, oxygen, suitable temperature, and time. Avoid saying all seeds need light to germinate. Light becomes especially important after leaves emerge.

Extension idea:

Try two seeds. Do they change on the same day? Draw both and compare what happens.
